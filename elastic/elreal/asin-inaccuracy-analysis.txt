I took on #1076 and did a deep dive, but the honest outcome is: it's not a quick fix — I diagnosed it thoroughly rather than force a hacky change.

What #1076 actually is

Through ~10 targeted experiments I narrowed it to a two-part conditioning bug:

1. asin under-resolves its deepest block. asin(0.5) and a clean pi/6 agree to 305 digits with identical block exponents — but per-block, blocks 0–17 are
perfect (320 digits each) while block 18 has only ~8 good significand digits. asin's value is still 305-accurate (the garbage sits at ~10⁻³⁰⁶), so it
passes the absolute check. Computing asin deeper doesn't help — it always returns 19 blocks with the last one garbage. The likely origin: asin =
atan(x/√(1−x²)), and the inner dense multi-block division under-resolves its last quotient block (connects to the dense-div work, #1068/#1072).
2. sin is well-conditioned for a clean perturbation but not for a wrong-significand deep block. sin(pi/6 + clean 2⁻ᴱ) tracks the perturbation perfectly
(self-consistent to 320), but sin(asin) saturates at ~234.

What I ruled out (so the next person doesn't repeat it)

Guard/working-precision blocks (no effect), re-priestRenorm (no effect), computing asin deeper (no effect), trimming the garbage block (no effect). It's
not a precision issue. I posted the full analysis to #1076 (https://github.com/stillwater-sc/universal/issues/1076#issuecomment-4733831721).

Keep it gated (as it is). A real fix needs dedicated work on the asin/atan/dense-div deep-block accuracy — reformulating the inverse-function chain to
produce a faithfully-decomposed tail — which is a meaningful investigation, not a one-liner. It's also pre-existing (eager gave ~239; my online conversion
added only ~5 digits), and only affects >234-digit inverse round-trips — a niche.

I deliberately stopped rather than ship a speculative fix to a bug I've shown resists the obvious remedies.

We took on the dense-division angle as you directed, and the result is a clean negative: it's not the cause.

Outcome of the dense-div investigation

Tracing asin = atan(div(0.5, sqrt(1−x²))) per-block at depth d vs d+8:
- sqrt, div, and atan are each self-consistent to 320 digits on every block — no under-resolved last block anywhere.
- asin(0.5) and pi6 are both 306 digits accurate vs the exact π/6 reference, both self-consistent to 320.

So the division is accurate. My earlier "asin's deepest block is garbage (8 figs)" reading was wrong — asin's block 18 is stable and accurate; it just
differs from pi6's block 18 because they're two equally-valid representations of π/6 diverging at the 10⁻³⁰⁶ precision limit. I've corrected the #1076
issue with this (retracting the dense-div hypothesis).

The real root cause (now pinned)

sin's Maclaurin-series machinery is decomposition/deepest-block sensitive: two inputs agreeing to 305 digits (each 306-accurate) yield sin = 234 vs 306 —
it amplifies a 10⁻³⁰⁶ deepest-block difference to 10⁻²³⁴ (effective condition number ~10⁷²). The amplification accrues through the term recurrence
(per-term agreement decays 305→15 over ~60 terms). Yet sin of a clean separate-block perturbation is well-conditioned, and t² is value-faithful — so it's a
specific conditioning quirk of the recurrence + infSum fold, not a precision or division issue.

Where that leaves it

This is a genuinely subtle numerical-conditioning bug, and the two angles I've now exhausted (precision/guard, and division accuracy) both came up empty. A
fix needs a focused look at the sin/cos series recurrence conditioning itself — a different and harder angle than either hypothesis. It remains gated and
fully documented in #1076 (which is the right state: a niche, pre-existing, >234-digit-only issue with a thorough paper trail).
